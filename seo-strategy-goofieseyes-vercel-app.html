Technical SEO

28 / 100
No robots.txt file Critical

Fetching https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/robots.txt returns a 404. Without a robots.txt, search engines have no crawl directives — and you miss the opportunity to point Googlebot to your sitemap. While Googlebot will still crawl without one, it's a missing trust signal and a missed indexing opportunity.

Create: /robots.txt
User-agent: * Allow: / Sitemap: https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/sitemap.xml
No sitemap.xml Critical

Fetching /sitemap.xml returns a 404. A sitemap is how you formally tell Google every URL you want indexed, their last-modified date, and their priority. For a single-page site it takes 5 minutes to create and guarantees faster indexing.

Create: /sitemap.xml
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?> <urlset xmlns="http://www.sitemaps.org/schemas/sitemap/0.9"> <url> <loc>https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/</loc> <lastmod>2026-03-31</lastmod> <priority>1.0</priority> </url> </urlset>
Canonical URL points to a domain that doesn't exist Critical

Your canonical tag reads: https://www.goofieseyes.com/. This domain is not registered — the site lives at goofieseyes.vercel.app. A canonical pointing to a non-existent URL tells Google to credit a dead domain with the authority this page earns. Fix immediately to ensure your Vercel URL gets full credit.

Replace in <head>
<link rel="canonical" href="https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/" />

📌 Note: If you purchase the domain goofieseyes.com in future, update the canonical to match — don't leave it pointing to Vercel after you migrate.

OG URL has wrong domain and includes /index.html High

The og:url tag reads https://www.goofieseyes.com/index.html — wrong domain, wrong URL format. Social platforms use og:url to de-duplicate shares. When someone shares your site, aggregated like/share counts will be split between URLs rather than consolidated.

Fix in <head>
<meta property="og:url" content="https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/" />
Page load: ~127 JPEG frames = potentially 15–25 MB uncompressed High

The scroll animation preloads all 127 JPEG frames before showing the site. Google's Core Web Vitals — especially Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) and Total Blocking Time — are ranking signals. A 15–25 MB initial payload will score poorly, especially on mobile connections. The loader hides the LCP element which is a good mitigation, but the bytes still need to transfer.

Recommendations:

① Re-export frames at 85% JPEG quality instead of the current max quality (-q:v 2 in FFmpeg). Aim for <80KB per frame = total <10MB.
② Consider serving WebP frames (40% smaller than JPEG at same quality) via a WebP-with-JPEG-fallback pattern.
③ Lazy-start: Begin the preload after the hero's first paint, not blocking it.

FFmpeg flag to reduce file size
ffmpeg -i video.mp4 -vf "fps=18,scale=1280:-2" -q:v 5 frames/frame_%04d.jpg # -q:v 5 = good quality, ~50-80KB/frame vs ~200KB at q:v 2 # scale=1280:-2 = halve the width (1920 is overkill for canvas on most screens)

On-Page SEO

Two H1 tags on the same page Critical

The page has two H1 elements: "Fine Art Photography" and "GOOFIE'S EYES.". Google officially supports multiple H1s, but in practice a single, keyword-rich H1 is the strongest on-page ranking signal you have. Two H1s dilute this signal. One should be demoted to H2 or converted into a styled span/div.

Recommended heading hierarchy
H1: "GoofiesEyes — Fine Art Photography" (one H1, above the fold) H2: "The Craft Behind Every Shot" (scroll animation section) H2: "The Numbers That Matter" (specs) H2: "The Work, Explored" (features) H2: "The Eye That Never Blinks" (Three.js section) H2: "Ready to See the World Differently?" (CTA)
Meta description too short — 102 chars (target: 150–160) High

Current: "GoofiesEyes Fine Art Photography. Portrait, landscape, editorial, and documentary visual storytelling." (102 chars). This won't fill the full snippet preview in Google, leaving click-through potential on the table. A strong description acts as an organic ad — use every character.

Suggested replacement (153 chars)
<meta name="description" content="Fine art photography by GoofiesEyes — portrait, landscape, editorial & documentary work. Commission a session or browse prints. Seeing the world differently, one frame at a time." />
Title tag is good but keyword placement could be improved High

Current title: "GoofiesEyes — Fine Art Photography Portfolio" (45 chars). Brand-first titles work once you have brand recognition — but unknown brands should lead with the descriptive keyword so people reading search results click before they know the brand. 45 chars is safely under the 60-char cutoff.

Suggested alternative
<title>Fine Art Photography Portfolio | GoofiesEyes</title> <!-- 48 chars — keyword-first, brand still present -->
No robots meta tag — crawl intent not declared High

There's no <meta name="robots"> tag. While Google indexes pages by default, explicitly declaring indexing intent is a best practice and prevents accidental noindex issues when staging environments inherit your code.

Add to <head>
<meta name="robots" content="index, follow" />

Even small numbers are better than zero.

Content Depth & E-E-A-T

20 / 100
Site has no actual photography — only a 3D camera model Critical

This is the most fundamental SEO issue for a photography portfolio. Google indexes content — for a photography site, that means actual photographs with descriptive file names, alt text, captions, and surrounding context. The current site shows only a scroll-animated 3D deconstruction of a camera, with no gallery of actual work.

Google Image Search alone drives enormous traffic for photographers. Without real images on the page, you're invisible in the channel most likely to drive clients to your door.

What to add
• A gallery section with 12–24 of your best photographs • Each image with: descriptive file name (e.g. "portrait-golden-hour-london.jpg") alt text (e.g. "Fine art portrait photography, golden hour London") • Captions with natural keyword use • Images in WebP format with JPEG fallback for page speed
No blog or multi-page content — single page cannot rank for multiple keywords Critical

Photography SEO research consistently shows that photographers with 30+ indexed pages consistently outrank those with 5 or fewer, even if the portfolio itself is stronger. A blog lets you target long-tail keywords like "best portrait photography tips", "how to find the right photographer for your wedding", or "fine art print sizes explained" — all of which drive qualified traffic.

You don't need to post daily. 2–4 in-depth articles per month targeting specific questions your ideal client types into Google will compound over 6–12 months into meaningful organic traffic.

Recommended content structure
/ → Portfolio homepage (current) /about → Your story, your process, your gear, your philosophy /portfolio → Gallery of work, filterable by type /blog → SEO articles (2–4 per month) /contact → Contact form + booking calendar /prints → Fine art prints for sale (if applicable)
No "About" page — major E-E-A-T gap High

Google's E-E-A-T framework (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) heavily rewards sites where there's a clear human author behind the content. An About page that shares your photography background, your process, your location, and your style gives Google the signals it needs to classify you as an expert, not a content farm.

It also helps potential clients decide whether to book you — human connection converts. A short bio and a face behind the lens makes a measurable difference to enquiry rates.

No contact or booking form — "Book a Session" links go nowhere High

All "Book a Session" and "View Gallery" CTAs link to #cta, which is a section with a CTA paragraph but no actual form, email link, or booking tool. A visitor who's ready to hire you has nowhere to go. This directly damages conversion rate, and high bounce rates from engaged-but-frustrated visitors are a negative engagement signal.

Quick fix (no backend needed)
<!-- Replace the CTA button with a mailto link --> <a href="mailto:hello@goofieseyes.com" class="btn-primary"> Book a Session </a> <!-- Or embed a free Calendly/Tally.so form in the CTA section -->

Schema Markup

35 / 100
Schema is WebSite only — missing Person and LocalBusiness High

The current JSON-LD only declares a WebSite type with name and URL. For a personal photography brand this misses two high-value schema types: Person (signals who the photographer is, enabling Knowledge Panel potential) and LocalBusiness (connects you to local Google Search and Maps results).

Replace the current JSON-LD block with this
<script type="application/ld+json"> [ { "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "WebSite", "name": "GoofiesEyes", "url": "https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/", "description": "Fine art photography portfolio — portrait, landscape, editorial, and documentary." }, { "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "Person", "name": "GoofiesEyes", "url": "https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/", "jobTitle": "Fine Art Photographer", "sameAs": [ "https://www.instagram.com/goofieseyes", "https://www.behance.net/goofieseyes" ] }, { "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "PhotographAction", "agent": { "@type": "Person", "name": "GoofiesEyes" }, "description": "Fine art portrait, landscape, editorial, and documentary photography." } ] </script>
No ImageGallery or ImageObject schema for photographs Medium

Once you add actual photographs to the site (see Content Depth section), wrap them in ImageObject schema. This gives Google metadata to display your images as rich results in Image Search, with your name and site as the source — driving brand recognition in search.

No og:image — links shared on social show no preview image Critical

When your site is shared on Instagram DMs, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, WhatsApp, or iMessage, platforms pull the og:image to generate a preview card. Without it, the link appears as plain text — no image, no context. For a photography brand, sharing a link with no image preview is a missed brand impression every single time.

Steps to fix
1. Create a 1200×630px social card image (use Canva or Figma) — Show one of your best photographs with the GoofiesEyes logo overlaid — Export as og-image.jpg and place it in your site root 2. Add to <head>: <meta property="og:image" content="https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/og-image.jpg" /> <meta property="og:image:width" content="1200" /> <meta property="og:image:height" content="630" /> <meta property="og:image:alt" content="GoofiesEyes Fine Art Photography" />
No Twitter/X Card meta tags High

Twitter/X uses its own meta tag format alongside OG tags. Without Twitter Card tags, X may fall back to a text-only link embed even if og:image is present (behavior varies). Add Twitter Card tags explicitly.

Add to <head>
<meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image" /> <meta name="twitter:title" content="GoofiesEyes — Fine Art Photography" /> <meta name="twitter:description" content="Fine art photography — portrait, landscape, editorial & documentary. Commission a session or browse prints." /> <meta name="twitter:image" content="https://goofieseyes.vercel.app/og-image.jpg" />
