Behind the Lens.

The context, the patience, and the thinking that goes into my favorite photographs.

Tobago 05:14 AM F/8, 1/125s, ISO 400

Chasing the Tobago Fog

There is a specific kind of silence that only exists before the sun breaks the horizon on an island. It's thick, humid, and completely still. I had scouted this exact slice of shoreline three days prior, measuring where the tide pulled against the black volcanic rocks.

My goal wasn't just to capture the landscape, but to capture the isolation. The way the fog rolls off the deep ocean and swallows the edges of the land. When I shoot landscapes, I'm rarely looking for the vibrant postcard view. I look for the mood. I look for the scene that makes you feel a slight chill.

"I didn't press the shutter when the sun came up. I waited until the cloud cover fractured enough to spill exactly one ray of light across the mist."

Technically, dealing with this much mist means fighting your camera's metering system, which desperately wants to turn everything a dull, muddy grey. I overexposed deliberately by one whole stop to protect the pure white of the fog, making sure the deep silhouettes of the shoreline rocks remained crushed in shadow.

Every time I look at this print, I don't just see the beach. I feel the cold, heavy air and remember the overwhelming quiet.

New York City Rainy Evening F/2.8, 1/60s, ISO 1600

Blurring the Avenues

Street photography is a delicate dance of anticipation. You cannot pose the street; you can only position yourself and wait for the theater to unfold. On this particular evening in Brooklyn, a sudden downpour had forced everyone under scaffolding, leaving the avenues slick and highly reflective.

The yellow cab is a cliché, but when treated correctly, it acts as a perfect geometric anchor amidst the chaos of urban movement. I dropped my shutter speed to 1/60th of a second—too slow for a perfectly sharp moving vehicle, but exactly right to introduce motion blur.

I wanted the energy of the city, not a static observation. The slight panning of the lens as the car passed smeared the background lights into a cinematic backdrop, contrasting sharply with the stark, stationary figure huddled with an umbrella on the right edge of the frame.
